The Case for a Four-Day Work Week

For decades, the five-day, forty-hour work week has been treated as an
immutable law of professional life. Yet a growing body of evidence suggests
that this structure is neither optimal for workers nor for the organisations
that employ them. A permanent shift to a four-day work week would improve
employee wellbeing, increase productivity, and reduce environmental costs —
without sacrificing economic output.

Proponents of longer hours often argue that more time at a desk equals more
work completed. Research tells a more complicated story. A landmark trial
conducted in Iceland between 2015 and 2019 found that productivity either
held steady or improved when the working week was cut to 35–36 hours. Workers
reported lower stress, reduced burnout, and better work-life balance. Microsoft
Japan reported a 40% productivity boost after trialling a four-day schedule in
2019. The mechanism is straightforward: people who are rested make fewer
mistakes, collaborate more effectively, and sustain concentration for longer.

Critics raise a legitimate concern about industries that depend on continuous
coverage — healthcare, emergency services, and logistics. A blanket policy
would indeed be impractical here. However, flexible scheduling, staggered
shifts, and compressed hours offer workable alternatives that preserve coverage
while still reducing total hours. The policy need not be uniform to be
worthwhile.

The environmental dividend is often overlooked in this debate. Fewer commuting
days translate directly into lower carbon emissions. A four-day week study in
the UK estimated a potential 21% reduction in carbon footprint from commuting
alone — a meaningful contribution as governments struggle to meet climate
targets.

The evidence is clear: the four-day work week is not a utopian fantasy but a
practical reform with documented benefits. Resistance rooted in tradition
should not override data. Governments and employers willing to pilot this model
will likely find that working less, done right, means achieving more.

Why the World's Fastest Trains Still Can't Beat the Plane — and What It Would
Take to Change That

High-speed rail is fast. Japan's Shinkansen network moves passengers at 320
kilometres per hour, and China's CR400 trains regularly hit 350 km/h on
commercial routes. Yet for journeys beyond about 800 kilometres, flying remains
the default for most travellers. Understanding why reveals a great deal about
the economics of infrastructure — and about what governments would need to
commit to if rail is ever to displace aviation at scale.

The core problem is distance decay. A train travelling at 300 km/h still needs
roughly three hours to cover 900 kilometres, and that figure excludes boarding
time, security checks, and transfers. A comparable flight, when you add airport
procedures, is often only marginally slower door-to-door — and it can reach
destinations that no rail line serves. For a traveller flying London to Athens,
there is simply no rail alternative that competes on time.

Cost compounds the problem. High-speed rail requires dedicated track — it
cannot share lines with slower freight or regional services without speed
penalties. The capital cost of a single kilometre of high-speed track in Europe
ranges from 10 to 80 million euros depending on terrain. Tunnelling through
mountains or crossing water vastly inflates that figure. These are investments
with multi-decade payback horizons, which makes them politically difficult even
when economically justifiable.

Where high-speed rail has succeeded, the conditions are specific: high
population density along the corridor, city-pair distances of 200 to 800
kilometres, and sustained government investment over decades. The Tokyo–Osaka
route, one of the busiest in the world, meets all three criteria. The proposed
HS2 route in the United Kingdom meets the first, partially meets the second,
and has already seen the third erode through political reversals.

For rail to genuinely challenge aviation on longer routes, three things would
need to change simultaneously: overnight sleeper networks would need to be
rebuilt at scale, cross-border ticketing and interoperability would need to
become seamless, and carbon pricing would need to make flying materially more
expensive. None of these is technically impossible. All of them are politically
hard.

The train may not beat the plane today. Whether it ever does depends less on
engineering than on political will.
